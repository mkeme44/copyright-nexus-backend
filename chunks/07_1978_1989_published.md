### Works Published January 1, 1978 - February 28, 1989: Transitional Period

**Context**: The Copyright Act of 1976 took effect January 1, 1978, changing many rules. However, notice was still required until the U.S. joined the Berne Convention on March 1, 1989.

**First Question**: Does the work have a copyright notice?

**If Copyright Notice IS Present**
- **Status**: In Copyright
- **Term**: Life of author + 70 years (or 95 years from publication for works made for hire)
- **Rights Statement**: In Copyright
- **URI**: https://rightsstatements.org/vocab/InC/1.0/
- **Confidence Level**: HIGH

**If NO Copyright Notice**

This is where it gets complicated. The 1976 Act provided a "cure" for omitted notice:

**Conditions That Could Save Copyright:**
1. Notice omitted from only a relatively small number of copies
2. Work was registered within 5 years of publication AND reasonable effort made to add notice
3. Notice was removed without authorization of copyright owner

**If No Cure Was Applied:**
- **Status**: Public Domain
- **Rights Statement**: No Copyright - United States

**If Cure Status is Unknown:**
- **Status**: Copyright Undetermined
- **Reason**: Cannot determine if corrective steps were taken
- **Rights Statement**: Copyright Undetermined
- **URI**: https://rightsstatements.org/vocab/UND/1.0/
- **Confidence Level**: LOW - Further research recommended

**Practical Guidance**
- If notice is present: Treat as in copyright
- If notice is absent: Status is uncertain without additional research
- Check Copyright Office records for registration within 5 years
- Contact copyright owner if identifiable

**Key Dates**
- January 1, 1978: 1976 Copyright Act takes effect
- March 1, 1989: U.S. joins Berne Convention, notice becomes optional

**Term Calculation for Works Made for Hire**
- 95 years from publication, OR
- 120 years from creation
- Whichever expires first
